Jamba Juice: Case Study

Tina Hong & 2 group members

Project Duration: Two Weeks

My Role: Researching, Interviewing, Persona Development, Storyboarding, Wireframing, Digital Mockups, Drink Details Web Page

Media Used: Sketch, Illustrator, Photoshop, Invision, Principle, Paper, & Pens


Initially, we discussed what we assumed to be their main challenges on by looking over their website, checking other competitors' websites, and reading reviews on the some of the local Jamba Juice stores as well as their competitors. One obvious painpoint was that their website was not responsive, making it difficult to order anything from a mobile phone.

We visited some of their locations to observe and interview some customers as well talk to their employees. We also set up a survey for people to fill out regarding their experiences at Jamba Juice.

SYNTHESIS

OPPORTUNITIES

After observing, interviewing, and surveying, we gathered all of our data and organized all of the painpoints into categories. We discussed the main problem that we wanted to tackle, and how we would try to solve the problem.



INSIGHTS


We coded all of our notes, to find patterns of frequencies and magnitudes in the customers' experiences. We discovered that:

  • Most customers were families with kids, or college-aged students with their friends.
  • Wait times for a drink were too long in the stores.
  • Customer service needed improvement.
  • There were not enough seating areas for customers in their stores.
  • Ordering a drink online was frustrating because they couldn't do it on their phone.
  • Customers did not want to download the app.
  • 80% of customers interviewed were unhappy with their online ordering system.
  • Most of the customers (over 80%) were not regulars.


PERSONA AND STORYBOARD

We used the information from our reseach findings to put together a User Persona and Storyboard.

PROBLEM STATEMENT

"Jamba Juice’s online ordering process is not responsive- making it impossible to pre-order a drink on their customers' phones- resulting in a decrease in sales. "

College students and parents with kids don’t have the time or patience to get in front of a computer to order a drink ahead of time.

Whil an app is available to download, most customers don’t download it because:

  • They don’t want to clutter the interface of their phone.
  • They don’t visit often enough to feel a need to download the app.


    • SOLUTION

    Design a responsive, playful, and easy user-flow so customers can order from their website.

    Ideation

    We discussed the possible ways our customer could order from the website. After researching current trends and popular apps and for college students, we concluded that a text/ chat format would be most appealing. We then proceeded to work out a user flow for the process.


    Flow Chart


    Style Guide

    At the same time, we also re-vamped the website's style, discussing what we liked about the current site, and what needed to be updated. We decided to keep the bright and playfulness of the current website, but make the website look cleaner and simpler so it did not look as juvenile.

    Quick Sketches

    We went from quick rough sketches, to low fidelity wireframing of layout options for the landing page, ordering format, and information page.


    Wireframes

    Visual Mock Ups

    After finalizing out low-fidelity wireframes, we made our digital mockups of the user flow.

    Once we worked on the visual mock-ups, each group member took on the task of coding a page of the ordering flow.

    We focused on a single item- a MANGO-A-GO-GO smoothie, and worked on a simple ordering flow for this drink. Our final coded page can be viewed HERE


    Conclusion

    Because my group members and I worked on this project together remotely, one of the challenges we faced were in communicating and keeping consistency throughout the pages. We were able to minimize this by having a shared style guide, Skyping regularly and having a group chat to address questions, concerns, offer support, and encouragement. Not having a background in web development was also constraint for us. Since one of the required deliverables were three coded pages, we all needed to be realistic in our finished goal, while challenging ourselves to the fullest. We learned eachothers' strengths and weaknesses, and had to maximize our efficiency by utilizing our strengths.
